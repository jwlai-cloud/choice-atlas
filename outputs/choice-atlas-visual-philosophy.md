# Cartographic Tenderness

This visual language treats consequential choices as a weather system rather than a problem to be solved. Routes are currents; certainty is not a bright answer but a small area of firm ground. The composition should let the eye feel a fork before it reads a label.

Ink contours provide the discipline of a map while fog, paper grain, and translucent washes preserve what cannot be known. Copper marks one pull, blue marks the other, and moss marks the shared terrain. These colors are evidence states and routes—not decoration—and they remain consistent across the product, diagrams, and film.

Space is used as an ethical boundary. Large quiet areas make room for the person’s own judgment; short captions never compete with the visual field. The most important statement is always one tension, not a recommendation.

Every line, gap, and label is deliberately measured: editorial, restrained, and meticulously crafted. Motion should look like a current or a breathing field, never like a generic loading effect. The result is a careful instrument for family-scale decisions—visually confident without pretending to be certain.
